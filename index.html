the digital—somatic design manifesto

This manifesto is for those who keep their phone’s typing clicks on; those who scroll their documents around whilst they think; those who savour the feel of a mechanical switch; those who miss the ubiquity of skeuomorphism and for those who relish in humane design.

It’s for software designers, web developers and interface creators who want their craft to inspire and comfort. It’s a set of guiding principles meant to quantify the innately, sensorially pleasing aspects of interactive design.

the physical-virtual

To bridge the gap between the real and intangible requires an understanding of what makes an item enjoyable to interact with. The best UX is created by a designer who lets themselves step away from their computer and discover what works in the real world.

the performance problem

Unresponsive software bogs down the mind as much as it slows down one’s workflow. Design lightweight. If a user fears that they’ll break the interface without due care, they'll feel uncomfortable.

the digital fidget

We feel the most at home using an interface when we can 'play' with it, as one might with a stray pencil or a loose fixture as their mind wanders. Remember that many users will interact with an interface in unexpected ways.

the free interaction

The “one free interaction” is a prospective design pattern conceptualised by Chris Noessel in 2008. The “free” interaction is a software feature that does nothing to the document’s content, but feels nice for the user to perform.

the other senses

Not everyone will want to hear audio flourishes or haptic feedback, but they can be just as important as visuals for how an interaction feels. Think beyond visual feedback.

the design future

Don't restrict yourself to imitating designs that have worked in the past. Everything that we consider ubiquitous had to be invented by someone first.
